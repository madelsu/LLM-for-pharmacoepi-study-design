# 🧠 Prompting Strategies

This document describes the two prompt engineering strategies used in this study to guide LLMs in supporting pharmacoepidemiological study design: **Active Prompting** and **Least-to-Most (LTM) Prompting**. Both strategies were applied across the same set of 10 pharmacoepidemiological concept questions, plus a final reasoning question (Q11).

---

## 📋 Overview

| Strategy | Core principle | Dependency between questions |
|---|---|---|
| Active Prompting | Uncertainty-driven exemplar selection with chain-of-thought examples | Independent per question |
| Least-to-Most (LTM) | Sequential decomposition where each answer builds on the previous | Explicitly chained |

---

## 1️⃣ Active Prompting

### Setup requirements

Before applying Active Prompting, the following preparation steps are required:

- **Seed questions:** At least 10–20 scenario-based seed questions should be provided per objective (e.g., examples spanning different disease areas and research types) to build training question sets.
- **Uncertainty metrics:** Define how to quantify the divergence of the *k* answers generated by the LLM (e.g., answer category consistency, keyword discrepancy rate, semantic similarity threshold).
- **Manual labelling rules:** Develop labelling templates for each research question (e.g., "definition of covariates").

---

### Questions and prompts

---

**Q1 — Study design**

> As a pharmacoepidemiologist, recommend the optimal study design type for the study titled `"{TITLE}"`. Consider all candidate designs (e.g., cohort, RCT, case-control).
>
> Constraints:
> 1. Align with CONSORT/STROBE guidelines
> 2. Prioritize feasibility in real-world settings
> 3. Explicitly state rejection reasons for alternative designs

*Chain-of-thought example:*
> Objective: Assess long-term effects of Drug X on risk
> - Step 1: Identify need for longitudinal exposure-outcome data → Cohort design
> - Step 2: Check feasibility of randomization → Reject RCT (lack of equipoise)
> - Step 3: Compare with case-control → Prefer cohort for temporal clarity

---

**Q2 — Index date**

> Define the index date of this study.
>
> Requirements:
> 1. For pharmacological studies: use first prescription date + 30-day washout
> 2. For surgical studies: use procedure date ± 7-day pre-op assessment
> 3. Flag potential immortal time bias sources

*Chain-of-thought example:*
> [Validation] Check FDA Sentinel Common Data Model, DARWIN, HMA-EMA for alignment

---

**Q3 — Inclusion and exclusion criteria**

> Generate inclusion and exclusion criteria for `"{TITLE}"`.
>
> Mandatory elements:
> 1. Explicit linkage to objective-specific endpoints
> 2. Use WHO International Classification of Functioning (ICF) framework
> 3. Flag criteria causing selection bias >20%

> Define temporal assessment windows for the inclusion/exclusion criteria identified in Q3.
>
> Constraints:
> 1. Align with EMA Guideline on GCP (Rev 3)
> 2. Use moving window approach for chronic conditions
> 3. Specify allowable overlaps (±5% timeline tolerance)

*Chain-of-thought example:*
> Objective: [state objective]
> Window Logic:
> 1. Baseline: [define]
> 2. Exclusion: [define]
>
> [Check] Apply inverse probability weighting for missing window data

## 2️⃣ Least-to-Most (LTM) Prompting

### Setup instructions

LTM prompting decomposes the study design task into a sequential chain of sub-problems. Each answer explicitly depends on the output of the previous question. The following principles govern the strategy:

- **Step 1:** Decompose the complex task into sequential sub-problems: study design → index date → inclusion/exclusion criteria → exposure/outcome definitions
- **Step 2:** Each subsequent sub-problem relies on the output of the previous step
- **Step 3:** For multi-objective studies, generate parameters separately for each objective, while permitting shared logic across objectives
- **Step 4:** Associate medical standard codes (e.g., ICD, CPT) only at the final step to ensure terminological consistency

---

### Questions and prompts

---

**Q1 — Study design**

> Based on the title `[TITLE]`, list all candidate study design types (e.g., cohort study, case-control study), select the most appropriate one, and justify your choice.

---

**Q2 — Index date**

> Define the index date for `[TITLE]` based on the `[Answer to Q1]` study design, indicating its specific definition and whether it is fixed or variable.

---

**Q3 — Inclusion and exclusion criteria**

> Develop inclusion criteria (e.g., age ≥18 years, confirmed disease) and exclusion criteria (e.g., past medical history, missing data) for `[TITLE]`, based on the `[Q1 answer]` design and `[Q2 answer]` index date.

---

**Q4 — Inclusion and exclusion assessment window**

> Based on the `[Q2 answer]` index date, identify the time window for assessing inclusion and exclusion criteria (e.g., a baseline period of 6 months before the index date, an exclusion period of 1 year after the index date) and specify its duration.

---

**Q5 — Study exposure**

> Based on the `[Q1 answer]` design and `[Q3 answer]` inclusion criteria, define the exposure factors (e.g., drug dose, exposure duration).

---

**Q6 — Study outcome**

> Based on the `[Q1 answer]` design, specify the primary and secondary outcome definitions (e.g., laboratory-confirmed diagnosis, imaging evidence).

---

**Q7 — Follow-up period**

> In conjunction with the `[Q2 answer]` index date and `[Q6 answer]` outcome definition, set the start and end of the follow-up period, whether right censoring is allowed, and the minimum follow-up length (e.g., 1 year).

---

**Q8 — Covariates**

> List the covariates to be adjusted for (e.g., age, sex, comorbidities, medication history) based on the `[Q5 answer]` exposure and `[Q6 answer]` outcome.

---

**Q9 — Covariate assessment window**

> Based on the `[Q2 answer]` index date, define the time window in which the covariates will be evaluated (e.g., laboratory data within 1 year prior to the index date).

---

**Q10 — Ontology coding**

> Convert diagnoses, exposures, outcomes, and covariates from `[Q3/Q5/Q6/Q8 answers]` to standard codes such as ICD-10, SNOMED CT, RxNorm, and LOINC.
